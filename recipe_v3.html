The Best Homemade Bread

Look no further for the BEST and simplest homemade Bread recipe made with just four simple pantry ingredients! It's the perfect artisan bread for sandwiches and it freezes well too!

A Brief History of Bread

The history of bread dates back over 30,000 years, evolving from simple flatbreads to a diverse array of bread types and flavors found in cuisines around the world today. The ancient Egyptians, around 3000 BCE, were the first to develop leavened bread using wild yeast and transformed the previously dense and flat loaves into lighter, airy bread with a distinct texture.

Bread is a versatile staple that goes well beyond just being a sandwich base. Whether stale or fresh, it can be turned into many different dishes. Leftover pieces can be toasted and crumbled into breadcrumbs for coating fried foods or topping casseroles. They can also be cut into cubes for crispy croutons in soups and salads. When soaked in a milk and egg mixture, bread becomes the foundation for classic French toast or a comforting bread pudding dessert.

Ingredients

Bread requires only four primary ingredients: flour, water, salt, and yeast. Other ingredients, such as sugars, fats, and various enrichments, are often added to enhance texture, flavor, and shelf life.

  • 1. Flour

    Flour is the foundational ingredient, providing the protein (gluten) that gives the bread its structure and elasticity.

    1. Bread Flour: Has a higher protein content than all-purpose, leading to a chewier, loftier bread.
    2. All-Purpose Flour: A versatile option with a moderate protein level, suitable for a variety of breads.
    3. Whole Wheat/Rye Flour: These flours offer more fiber and flavor but produce a denser loaf due to less gluten development.

  • 2. Liquid (Water, Milk, Eggs)

    Liquid hydrates the flour, enabling the formation of gluten and dissolving other ingredients.

    1. Water: The most common liquid in lean doughs, essential for activating yeast.
    2. Milk: Adds richness, helps the crust brown, and creates a softer crumb.
    3. Eggs: Provide structure, flavor, color, and enrichment.

  • 3. Leavening Agent

    Leavening produces the gas (carbon dioxide) that makes the dough rise, creating an open, light texture.

    1. Yeast (Active Dry or Instant): Feeds on the sugars in the dough and produces CO2. Ensure the water temperature is correct (105-115¬∞F for active dry) to activate it effectively.
    2. Sourdough Starter: A natural leavening agent made from a fermented mixture of flour and water.
    3. Baking Soda/Powder: Used in "quick breads" and chemically reacts to produce lift without requiring proofing time.

  • 4. Salt

    Salt is crucial for flavor enhancement and controls the yeast's activity, ensuring the dough does not rise too quickly. It also strengthens the gluten network.

  • Optional Additions

    These ingredients are used to customize the bread's characteristics:

    1. Sweeteners (Sugar, Honey, Molasses): Feed the yeast and add flavor, moisture, and help with crust browning.
    2. Fats (Butter, Oil): Add moisture, soften the crumb, and improve the keeping properties of the bread.
    3. Inclusions: Seeds, nuts, fruits, and herbs can be added for texture, flavor, and nutritional value.

How Bread is Made

While methods vary, the basic process for yeast-leavened bread involves a few key steps:

  • Mixing Ingredients: Combining primary ingredients to form a dough.
  • Kneading: Working the dough to develop the gluten network.
  • First Rise (Bulk Fermentation): Allowing the dough to rest in a warm place while yeast ferments.
  • Shaping: Punching down the dough and shaping it into a loaf.
  • Second Rise (Proofing): A final rise after shaping, before baking.
  • Baking: The heat causes the gas cells to expand, creating the final loaf.

About 30 minutes before baking, preheat your oven to 450¬∞F (230¬∞C). Place a Dutch oven (a heavy, lidded cooking pot) inside the oven to preheat as well. The hot Dutch oven creates steam during baking, which is essential for developing a crispy, golden crust on your bread.

Step 5: Shape the Dough

Carefully turn the fermented dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Gently shape it into a round ball by folding the edges toward the center. Using a sharp knife or bread lame (scoring tool), make a ¬Ω-inch deep cut across the top of the dough in a single slash or cross pattern. This scoring allows the bread to expand properly during baking and creates that professional bakery look.

Step 7: First Bake with Lid

Carefully remove the hot Dutch oven from the oven. Place your shaped dough inside (use parchment paper if you're worried about sticking). Cover with the lid and bake at 450¬∞F for 30 minutes. The covered pot traps steam, creating that beautiful, crispy crust.

Step 8: Second Bake Without Lid

After 30 minutes, carefully remove the hot lid (watch for steam!) and continue baking for another 15-30 minutes until the bread is deep golden brown. The longer you bake without the lid, the darker and crispier the crust will be. Look for an internal temperature of 205-210¬∞F using a bread thermometer.

Step 9: Cool Completely

Remove the bread from the Dutch oven and place it on a wire cooling rack. This is crucial‚Äîdo not cut into the bread while it's hot! As it cools, the interior continues to cook and set. Let it cool for at least 1-2 hours before slicing. This allows the crumb structure to stabilize, resulting in cleaner slices.
